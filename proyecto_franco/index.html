Fragmented Reality

What is truth?

Mixed media on paper  ·  2026

Concept

Reality has never been a singular, stable object of perception. This work enters that tension — the gap between what the eye receives and what the mind constructs — and presses on it. Drawing from phenomenological philosophy, the piece stages the viewer's own perceptual apparatus as its subject: the act of seeing is itself what is being seen.

The central question — what is truth? — is posed not rhetorically but formally: the composition refuses to resolve into any unified reading, enacting the very impossibility it describes.

Visual Analysis

The composition is organized around a central dark mass — an organic, tree-like form rendered in dense blacks and browns that radiates outward into branching extensions. This anchoring element provides structural gravity, but the work refuses coherence: scattered across the field are autonomous geometric fragments — triangles, diamonds, irregular polygons — rendered in high-saturation primaries and secondaries that bear no spatial or tonal relation to the central form. They float, untethered, in a white ground that functions not as empty background but as the condition of possibility for all the visual events occurring within it.

The chromatic logic is deliberately dissonant. The vivid yellows, magentas, greens, and oranges of the peripheral fragments insist on their own independent presence, resisting subordination to the dominant form at center. This tension between the organic and the geometric, the contained and the dispersed, enacts the cognitive experience of perception under pressure: we reach for pattern, for wholeness — and the work refuses it.

The triptych structure of the support itself — the work's ground is divided into panels — introduces a meta-commentary: reality, the piece seems to argue, is always already fragmented before we begin to look at it. Symbolically, the central tree reads simultaneously as a living system and as an exposed skeleton — something that once held leaves, held color, held coherence, and has been stripped to its essential, unresolvable structure.

02 / 03

Lost in Time

Moments fade away...

Paper collage  ·  2026

Concept

Time cannot be held. This work begins from that impossibility and asks what remains when the present becomes the past — what residue, what trace, what negative space marks the place where a moment existed and no longer does. Memory, the work proposes, is not a recording but an editing: what we retain is already shaped by what we have already lost.

Visual Analysis

The work deploys paper — inherently fragile, already marked by time in its manufacture — as a carrier of temporal meaning. The act of cutting is central: the artist removes material to create form, and the voids left behind are compositionally equal to the pieces that remain. Stars appear twice in the composition: once as cut-away negative spaces within the beige sheets, once as positive forms set free from their ground. The moon, rendered in warm gold, emerges from its parent sheet as a crescent — a temporal symbol so ancient as to precede recorded history.

The palette is deliberately restrained: beige, cream, an accent blue, gold. These are the colors of old paper, of documents worn by time, of dawn and dusk — the liminal temporal zones where one state transitions into another. The blue rectangle with its circular void reads as a window into night sky; the circular form, removed, floats nearby as a separate object — the same shape, the same scale, but now unmoored. This separation of a thing from its origin is the formal correlate of memory: the trace persists, but the context that gave it meaning has receded.

The compositional arrangement is horizontal and spacious, resisting dramatic urgency. Time here is not a crisis — it is the quiet, irreversible passage of what was once present into what is merely recalled. The scattered stars at the base of the composition suggest that time does not flow in a single direction but disperses: outward, downward, into the margins.

03 / 03

Chaos Within

Can you silence the storm?

Digital collage / Mixed media  ·  2026

Concept

The interior life does not arrange itself for presentation. This work refuses the demand that inner experience be calm, legible, or resolved, and instead inhabits the turbulence directly — not as pathology to be diagnosed or disorder to be corrected, but as an authentic condition of consciousness worth attending to. In asking can you silence the storm?, the piece does not presume an answer; it holds the question open as a space of honest self-inquiry.

Visual Analysis

The work is organized as a triptych — three panels sharing chromatic and thematic logic while maintaining distinct visual territories. The warm palette of deep reds, burnt oranges, and aged golds dominates all three registers, creating a temperature of intensity that operates affectively before it operates iconographically: the viewer is pulled into heat before they begin to read.

Individual panels develop specific metaphorical arguments. A figure whose cranial architecture gives way to fire — releasing not only destruction but butterflies — complicates any simple reading of chaos as mere ruin. The butterfly's iconographic association with metamorphosis suggests that the disorder depicted is not purely destructive but potentially generative: chaos as the precondition of transformation. A solitary figure ascending stone stairs beneath a cosmic clockface stages the individual's movement through time as simultaneously heroic and absurd — progress against an indifferent scale. A fragmented sculptural face — cracked, held by hands that may be assembling or preventing further collapse — frames identity as something always under pressure, always in process.

Formally, the piece draws from the tradition of Surrealist photomontage and contemporary collage aesthetics, deploying juxtapositions of scale, texture, and symbolic register as its primary compositional strategy. Meaning does not inhere in any single element but emerges from the productive dissonance between them — a formal logic that mirrors the thematic: chaos is not the absence of structure, but a different, more difficult kind of order.

About

The Artist

Franco Yazigi is a visual artist currently developing a practice at the intersection of conceptual inquiry and material experimentation. Working across drawing, collage, and digital media, their work investigates the unstable boundaries between perception and reality, the subjective experience of time, and the visual representation of internal emotional states.

Informed by phenomenology, Surrealist traditions, and contemporary visual culture, the work asks how images can carry psychological weight — and what it means to make visible what ordinarily remains hidden. These works were produced as part of an ongoing exploration of what it means to see, remember, and feel.
